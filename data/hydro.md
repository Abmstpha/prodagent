# Hydropower

Hydropower generates electricity by passing water through turbines, usually from a
reservoir behind a dam or from run-of-river flow. It is the largest renewable source
of electricity: about 1,410 GW of installed capacity produced roughly 4,300 TWh in
2023, around 14% of global electricity — more than all other renewables combined
until solar and wind overtook it in combined output in recent years.

Hydropower plants have capacity factors of 40% to 60% and a global weighted-average
LCOE of about $0.057 per kWh in 2023. Unlike solar and wind, reservoir hydropower is
dispatchable: output can be raised or lowered in minutes, and pumped-storage hydro is
the world's largest form of energy storage, holding over 90% of grid-scale storage
capacity. The Three Gorges Dam in China is the largest power station in the world at
22.5 GW.

The main constraints are geography — most easily exploitable sites in developed
countries are already used — high upfront construction cost, multi-year build times,
ecological impact on rivers and fish migration, and vulnerability to drought, which
reduced global hydro output notably in 2023.
